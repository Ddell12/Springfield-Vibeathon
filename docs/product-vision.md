# Product Vision — Bridges

## 1. Vision & Mission

### Vision Statement

A world where the people closest to a problem — the parents, therapists, and teachers who live it every day — have the power to build their own solutions, no developer required.

### Mission Statement

Bridges gives ABA therapists, speech-language pathologists, and parents of children with autism the ability to create personalized therapy tools by describing what they need in plain language, powered by AI that already speaks their world.

### Founder's Why

Desha is a full-stack AI developer and the parent of Ace, a child with Level 1 ASD. He's lived both sides of the gap this product exists to close — the technical side, where he can build anything from scratch, and the parent side, where he's seen firsthand how inaccessible, expensive, and one-size-fits-all current therapy options are.

ABA therapy is under scrutiny. It's expensive, hard to access, and leaves parents feeling lost between sessions. The therapist gives you a paper handout and says "practice this at home" — and then you're on your own for 23 hours until the next appointment. Desha has downloaded dozens of autism apps for Ace. None of them fit his son's specific goals, specific motivators, or specific developmental level. Every kid on the spectrum is different, but every tool treats them the same.

The timing is personal and technical. Ace is at a critical developmental age. AI can finally handle natural language well enough to understand what a parent means when they say "morning routine" or what a therapist means when they say "discrete trial training." Desha has the rare combination of deep domain empathy and the full-stack AI skills to build the solution. He joined OneDay MBA to learn the business and go-to-market skills needed to turn this into something sustainable — because changing lives requires more than good code.

### Core Values

**Speak their language, not ours.** Every word in Bridges should feel familiar to the person using it. A parent says "picture cards" — the AI knows that means a visual communication board. A BCBA says "DTT" — the AI knows that means discrete trial training with trial-by-trial data collection. We never expose technical jargon. If a user sees the word "component," "database," or "deploy," something went wrong.

**Every child is different. Every tool should be too.** The entire point of Bridges is that generic doesn't work. A token board for a child who loves trains looks different from one for a child who loves dinosaurs. A visual schedule for a morning routine is different from one for a therapy session. We build tools that adapt to the child, not tools that force the child to adapt.

**Small wins are real wins.** In the autism therapy world, progress is measured in moments — a child requesting a snack for the first time, maintaining eye contact for two seconds longer, tolerating a new food texture. Bridges celebrates these moments and treats them with the weight they deserve. Our UX reflects this: we never rush, we never overwhelm, and we always acknowledge progress.

**Parents are partners, not passengers.** Parents of autistic children are often treated as observers in their child's therapy. Bridges treats them as builders, decision-makers, and experts on their own child. The tools they create are real therapy tools, not toys or approximations. A parent who builds a communication board in Bridges has created something as valid as what a therapist would build.

**Ship fast, stay simple.** Complexity is the enemy of accessibility. Every feature we add must pass the test: "Would a stressed parent at 11pm with 10 minutes of energy left be able to figure this out?" If the answer is no, we simplify until it's yes.

### Strategic Pillars

**Domain intelligence is the moat.** Bridges' competitive advantage isn't the AI — it's the AI that already knows therapy. Pre-loaded knowledge of ABA terminology, speech therapy interventions, IEP goals, and developmental milestones means the AI produces useful results from the first interaction. General-purpose tools can't match this without extensive prompting.

**The parent's workflow comes first, always.** When there's a conflict between what's technically elegant and what's simple for a parent, the parent wins. This means: no configuration screens, no multi-step setup, no accounts required to try it. Describe what you need, get a working tool.

**Every shared tool is a growth loop.** When a therapist shares a Bridges-built tool with a parent, or a parent shares one with another parent in a Facebook group, that's organic distribution. The sharing mechanism is as important as the building mechanism.

**Credibility through utility, not credentials.** Bridges earns the trust of therapists not by claiming clinical authority but by being genuinely useful. If a BCBA saves 3 hours a week on material prep, she'll recommend it. We don't need to be a medical device — we need to be the tool therapists can't live without.

### Success Looks Like

Twelve months from now, Bridges has 500 active families and 50 therapists creating tools weekly. The phrase "just Bridge it" has started appearing in ABA therapist Facebook groups when someone asks how to make a custom visual schedule. Three ABA clinic chains have adopted team plans. Parents are sharing Bridges-built tools in autism support communities and the most popular ones — a customizable visual schedule, a token board builder, and a communication board with text-to-speech — have been used by thousands of families. Monthly recurring revenue has hit $5,000 from premium subscriptions. Most importantly: parents are reporting that their children are using Bridges-built tools at home, and therapists are seeing measurable carry-over from home practice to clinic sessions. The gap between therapy and home is closing.

### Pain Points

**1. Generic tools for non-generic kids (Severity: Critical, Frequency: Daily)**
Every autism app, every set of picture cards, every pre-made visual schedule assumes a "standard" autistic child who doesn't exist. Parents download apps, try them for a day, and abandon them because the content doesn't match their child's goals, interests, or ability level. Currently: parents adapt generic materials by hand (crossing out irrelevant items, adding photos) or just don't practice at home. Consequence: kids miss out on the 90% of waking hours that happen outside therapy.

**2. The therapy-home black hole (Severity: Critical, Frequency: Weekly)**
What happens in therapy stays in therapy. Parents get a verbal summary at pickup and maybe a photocopied handout. There's no mechanism for practicing the same activities at home, no way to track if home practice is effective, and no way for therapists to see what happened between sessions. Currently: parents wing it, feel guilty, and often give up. Consequence: lack of generalization — skills learned in clinic don't transfer to home.

**3. Material prep burnout for therapists (Severity: High, Frequency: Weekly)**
BCBAs and SLPs spend 3–5 hours per week creating, printing, laminating, and organizing physical therapy materials. When a child masters a goal and targets change, the materials are remade from scratch. Currently: therapists absorb this as unpaid prep time. Consequence: therapist burnout, less time for actual therapy and parent training.

**4. Therapy cost and access barriers (Severity: Critical, Frequency: Ongoing)**
ABA therapy costs $50–100/hour, is often waitlisted for 6–12 months, and may not be covered by insurance. Many families — especially in rural areas — simply can't access it. Currently: families wait, or go without. Consequence: kids miss the critical early intervention window (ages 2–5) that produces the best outcomes.

### Current Alternatives & Competitive Landscape

**Proloquo2Go ($250):** The gold standard AAC app. Excellent for high-support-needs users but expensive, complex to configure, and designed as a full communication replacement system — not a quick tool builder. Parents find it overwhelming. Not competition for Bridges — serves a different, higher-support-needs user.

**CentralReach / Catalyst:** Enterprise ABA practice management platforms. Built for clinic billing, scheduling, and compliance — not for parents. Therapists use them because their clinic mandates it, not because they love the UX. Expensive ($200+/seat/month). Not designed for material creation.

**Teachers Pay Teachers:** Marketplace for downloadable educational materials. Therapists buy PDF worksheets for $2–5 each. Static, not customizable, not digital-native. Requires printing and often laminating. Popular because nothing better exists, not because it's good.

**Speech Blubs, AutiSpark, generic "autism apps":** Consumer apps that promise to help with speech and development. Content is generic, gamified, and not connected to any actual therapy plan. Parents download them hoping they'll help, use them for a week, and move on.

**Google Sheets + laminator + velcro:** The real competitor. Most therapists track data in spreadsheets and make physical materials by hand. Most parents get photocopies. It "works" in the sense that it's been done this way for decades, but it's slow, not portable, not adaptable, and creates no feedback loop.

**Doing nothing:** Many parents, overwhelmed by options and unsure what's correct, simply don't practice between sessions. This is the most common "alternative" and the one Bridges most directly replaces.

### Key Assumptions to Validate

1. **Parents will build tools if we make it easy enough.** We assume the barrier is complexity, not motivation. To validate: launch beta with 10 parents, measure how many create at least one tool in the first session without support.

2. **AI can produce therapy-appropriate content without clinical supervision.** We assume Claude, with the right system prompts and RAG context, can generate tools that therapists would endorse. To validate: have 3 BCBAs review 20 AI-generated tools and rate them for clinical appropriateness.

3. **Therapists will share Bridges-built tools with parents.** We assume therapists see Bridges as an extension of their practice, not a threat to their role. To validate: interview 5 therapists after using Bridges for 2 weeks — do they feel comfortable sharing generated tools with families?

4. **Config-based generation is sufficient for therapy tools.** We assume therapy tools are a finite set of patterns (visual schedules, token boards, communication boards, data sheets) that can be represented as configurations rather than arbitrary code. To validate: catalog the therapy tool types requested by the first 50 users — do they fit the pre-built component library?

5. **Parents will return after the first session.** We assume the initial magic moment creates enough value for repeat usage. To validate: track 7-day and 30-day retention rates. Target: 40% 7-day, 20% 30-day.

6. **Text-to-speech on communication boards is a meaningful feature, not a gimmick.** We assume hearing their request spoken aloud is motivating for children and useful for parents. To validate: A/B test tools with and without TTS — measure child engagement and parent satisfaction.

7. **The freemium model works for this audience.** We assume parents under financial strain from therapy costs will upgrade for advanced features. To validate: track free-to-paid conversion rate. If <2% at 90 days, reconsider the pricing model.

## 3. Product Strategy

### Product Principles

**Thirty seconds to value.** From the moment a user describes what they need to the moment they're looking at a working tool — 30 seconds or less. Every design decision is evaluated against this clock. If adding a feature slows down the path to the first generated tool, it doesn't ship.

**The AI is the expert, the user is the authority.** The AI knows what a token board is and how to structure one. But the parent knows their child — what motivates them, what triggers them, what they're working on right now. Bridges combines AI expertise with parent authority. The AI suggests, the parent decides.

**Nothing to configure, everything to customize.** There should be zero setup before creating a first tool. No onboarding wizard, no preference screens, no workspace configuration. But after a tool is generated, every element should be tweakable through conversation — "make the pictures bigger," "add a reward screen at the end," "change the colors to blue."

**Share as the default action.** The most important thing that happens after a tool is built is that it reaches the person who needs it — the child, the parent, another therapist. Sharing should be one tap, produce a link, and work on any device. No app store, no install, no account required to view.

**Pre-built is better than generated.** Therapy tools follow known patterns. A config-based approach (AI selects and configures pre-built components) is more reliable, faster, and more consistent than generating arbitrary code. The AI's job is to understand intent and produce the right configuration — the components handle the rendering.

**Data should flow, not sit.** When a child uses a tool at home, the data (trials completed, accuracy, time spent) should flow back to wherever it's useful — the parent's dashboard, the therapist's view, an exportable report. Data that sits in one place is wasted.

### Market Differentiation

Bridges occupies a unique position: it's the only tool that combines vibe-coding accessibility (describe what you want, get a working product) with deep therapy domain knowledge (the AI already understands ABA, speech therapy, and developmental milestones).

Existing therapy tools like CentralReach and Catalyst are built for clinical operations — billing, scheduling, compliance reporting. They assume the user has clinical training. Parents can't use them. Existing consumer apps like Speech Blubs and AutiSpark are one-size-fits-all — the same generic content for every child. Existing vibe-coding tools like Replit, Lovable, and Bolt assume the user thinks like a developer — they understand components, databases, and deployment. A parent or therapist can't use them.

Bridges sits at the intersection of these categories. It has the creation power of a vibe-coding platform, the clinical awareness of a therapy tool, and the simplicity of a consumer app. The moat is the therapy domain intelligence: the RAG knowledge base of ABA terminology, IEP goal banks, speech therapy targets, and developmental milestones that makes the AI feel like it already knows your child's world. A generic tool can't replicate this without extensive manual prompting — and the whole point is that these users shouldn't have to prompt.

### Magic Moment Design

The magic moment is the instant a parent or therapist sees that Bridges *understood* what they needed — without them having to translate their knowledge into technical language.

For this to happen reliably, three things must be true:

1. **The AI must understand therapy language.** When a parent says "requesting snacks" the AI must know this means a manding activity and generate an appropriate communication board. When a therapist says "DTT data sheet" the AI must know the structure of discrete trial training. This requires the therapy RAG knowledge base to be loaded and working before the first user interaction.

2. **Generation must be fast.** Under 30 seconds from description to visible tool. This means the config-based approach is essential — the AI produces a JSON config, and pre-built React components render it instantly. No sandboxed code generation, no build step, no loading spinners that last more than a few seconds.

3. **The generated tool must work on the user's device immediately.** No download, no install, no "open in app store." A shareable link that works on any phone, tablet, or computer. The tool should be interactive (tappable, swipeable) the moment it loads.

The shortest path from first visit to magic moment: land on homepage → type description → (optional: answer 1 follow-up question) → see working tool → tap/interact with it → share it. No account required. No onboarding. No sign-up gate before the first tool is generated.

### MVP Definition

**In Scope — Must ship for hackathon demo:**

**Conversational tool builder.** The core interaction loop: user types what they need in plain language, AI responds with clarifying questions if needed, generates a tool config, and renders it live. Powered by Vercel AI SDK + Claude + therapy-aware system prompt + RAG knowledge base. This is the heart of Bridges.

**5 pre-built therapy tool components.** Visual schedule, token board, communication board (with ElevenLabs TTS), choice board, and first-then board. Each component renders from a JSON configuration and is interactive (tappable, draggable where appropriate). These cover the most commonly used therapy tools.

**Therapy-aware RAG knowledge base.** Google gemini-embedding-001 embeddings stored in Convex vector search. Pre-loaded with ABA terminology, common intervention patterns, therapy tool structures, and developmental milestones. This is what makes the AI "speak therapy."

**Text-to-speech on communication boards.** When a child taps a picture card, the corresponding request is spoken aloud via ElevenLabs. This is the killer demo moment — "my son tapped 'I want crackers' and the app said it out loud."

**Shareable links.** Every generated tool gets a unique URL that works on any device. No account required to view or use a shared tool. Share via link, QR code, or native share dialog.

**Basic tool customization via chat.** After a tool is generated, the user can refine it through conversation: "make the pictures bigger," "add another snack option," "change the reward to iPad time." The AI updates the config and the tool re-renders.

**Tool persistence.** Generated tools are saved in Convex so users can return to them later. Accessible via a simple "My Tools" list.

### Explicitly Out of Scope

**User accounts and authentication (hackathon).** Deferred to final phase. Tools are accessed via shareable links during the hackathon. Clerk auth will be added as one of the last build tasks to avoid E2E testing friction during development.

**Payment processing.** No paywall for the hackathon demo. Stripe integration is post-launch. The freemium model's free tier is sufficient for the demo.

**Video analysis (Twelve Labs).** Uploading and analyzing therapy session videos is a powerful feature but adds significant complexity. Deferred to v2. Timeline: reconsider after 100 active users.

**Therapist-to-parent handoff workflow.** The flow where a therapist builds a tool and assigns it to a parent's account requires auth, roles, and a relationship model. Deferred until auth is implemented.

**Community tool library.** A marketplace where therapists share tools they've built requires moderation, search, and curation. Deferred to v2. Timeline: reconsider after 50 therapists are creating tools.

**Progress data and analytics.** Tracking how often a tool is used, trial accuracy, and skill progression. Requires data collection within tools and a reporting layer. Deferred until the core builder is validated. Timeline: v2.

**Multi-language support.** The therapy knowledge base and AI interactions are English-only for the MVP. Timeline: reconsider when expanding beyond US market.

**Screenshot-to-code (paper digitization).** Photographing existing paper therapy materials and converting them to digital tools. Cool feature, not core. Timeline: v2.

### Core User Flows

**Flow 1: Parent Creates a Tool**
Trigger: Parent opens Bridges (no account needed).
1. Parent sees a clean chat interface with prompt: "Tell me what you need — I'll handle the technical part."
2. Parent types: "I need something to help my son practice requesting snacks."
3. AI asks: "What are his favorite snacks? I'll make picture cards for each one."
4. Parent responds: "Goldfish crackers, apple slices, yogurt tubes."
5. AI generates a communication board config and renders it: picture cards for each snack, "I want ___" sentence strip, TTS button.
6. Parent sees the live tool and interacts with it (taps a card, hears "I want goldfish crackers").
7. Parent says "can you make the pictures bigger?" → AI updates config → tool re-renders.
8. Parent taps "Share" → gets a link → sends to their phone.
Success: Working, personalized tool in under 60 seconds.

**Flow 2: Therapist Creates and Shares a Tool**
Trigger: BCBA opens Bridges to create materials for her caseload.
1. Therapist types: "Build me a 5-token board with a choice of reinforcers at the end."
2. AI generates a token board config: 5 token slots, 3 reinforcer options (with placeholder images), reset button, celebration animation on completion.
3. Therapist says "change the reinforcers to iPad, swing, and bubbles."
4. AI updates config → tool re-renders.
5. Therapist taps "Share" → gets a link → texts it to the child's parents.
6. Parents open the link on their phone → tool works immediately.
Success: Tool created in 30 seconds, shared in 10, works on parent's device instantly.

**Flow 3: User Browses Templates**
Trigger: User isn't sure what to build.
1. User opens Bridges and sees "Not sure where to start? Browse templates."
2. User sees template categories: Morning Routines, Communication, Behavior Support, Academic Skills.
3. User taps "Communication" → sees pre-built templates: Snack Requesting Board, Feelings Check-In, Yes/No Board.
4. User taps "Snack Requesting Board" → it loads pre-configured → user taps "Customize."
5. Chat opens with the template pre-loaded. User says "change the snacks to my son's favorites."
6. AI updates the config → tool re-renders with customized content.
Success: User who didn't know what to ask for now has a personalized tool.

### Risks

**1. Clinical accuracy concerns (Likelihood: High, Impact: High)**
If the AI generates a tool with inappropriate content, incorrect therapy structure, or harmful recommendations, it could damage trust irreparably. Mitigation: The AI builds tools, not therapy plans. Tools are visual aids and practice materials, not clinical interventions. System prompts explicitly state this. Include a disclaimer: "Bridges creates practice tools, not clinical advice. Always follow your therapist's guidance."

**2. Therapy community skepticism (Likelihood: Medium, Impact: High)**
BCBAs and SLPs may see AI-generated tools as threatening their professional role or producing clinically questionable content. Mitigation: Position Bridges as a time-saver for therapists, not a replacement. Lead with the therapist use case (material prep reduction) before the parent use case (independence from therapists).

**3. Limited component library feels restrictive (Likelihood: Medium, Impact: Medium)**
If users frequently request tool types not in the pre-built library, the config-based approach hits its ceiling. Mitigation: Start with the 5 most commonly used therapy tools. Track every request that doesn't fit the library. If more than 20% of requests fall outside the library at 90 days, prioritize expanding the component set.

**4. Parent engagement drops after initial novelty (Likelihood: Medium, Impact: High)**
Parents create one or two tools, the novelty wears off, and they stop returning. Mitigation: Focus on tools that require ongoing use (daily visual schedules, session-by-session data collection) rather than one-time creations. Build habit through daily utility, not gamification.

**5. Hackathon time constraint (Likelihood: High, Impact: Medium)**
Building a working demo in 4 days with 5 therapy components, RAG, TTS, and a polished UI is ambitious. Mitigation: Ruthless scope management. The demo must show 1 complete flow (parent creates a communication board with TTS) working perfectly. Other components can be partially implemented. A perfect demo of one flow beats a buggy demo of five.

**6. ElevenLabs latency (Likelihood: Low, Impact: Medium)**
If TTS takes more than 1–2 seconds to respond, the magic moment is broken. Mitigation: Pre-generate common phrases. Cache TTS results. Show a subtle loading indicator while audio generates.

**7. Regulatory creep (Likelihood: Low, Impact: High)**
If Bridges is perceived as a medical device or therapy delivery system, it could face HIPAA, COPPA, or FDA regulatory requirements. Mitigation: Frame as a tool builder, not a therapy provider. Don't store clinical data. Don't make clinical claims. Include disclaimers. Consult a healthcare attorney before storing any personally identifiable information about children.

## 4. Brand Strategy

### Positioning Statement

For parents of autistic children and therapy professionals who need custom therapy tools but can't code, Bridges is the AI-powered tool builder that lets you create personalized visual supports, communication boards, and practice activities by simply describing what you need. Unlike generic autism apps that treat every child the same, Bridges creates tools tailored to your specific child because no two kids on the spectrum are alike.

### Brand Personality

Bridges is the warm, patient person you wish was in every therapist's waiting room. The one who sits down next to a confused parent and says "Tell me what you're working on — I'll help you figure this out." They never rush. They never use jargon. They never make you feel stupid for not knowing something.

They have deep expertise — they know what a discrete trial looks like, what a token economy is, how to structure a visual schedule for maximum effectiveness — but they wear that knowledge lightly. They share it through helpful suggestions, not lectures. They'd rather show you than tell you.

They celebrate the small things. When a child uses a tool for the first time, that's worth acknowledging. When a parent builds their first tool, that's worth celebrating. They understand that in this world, small wins are everything.

They dress practically — clean, organized, professional but not stiff. Think: the therapist who wears comfortable shoes because she'll be on the floor with kids all day, but whose materials are always perfectly organized.

They would never: use clinical jargon without explaining it, rush someone through a process, celebrate with over-the-top animations, use corporate buzzwords, or make a parent feel like they're doing it wrong.

### Voice & Tone Guide

**Voice (constant):** Warm, clear, knowledgeable, patient. Uses plain language. First person plural when appropriate ("Let's build something"). Second person when guiding ("Tell me what you need").

**Tone shifts by context:**

| Context | Tone | DO | DON'T |
|---------|------|----|-------|
| Onboarding | Welcoming, low-pressure | "Tell me what you need — I'll handle the technical part." | "Welcome to Bridges! Let's get you set up with your account preferences." |
| Tool generation | Confident, collaborative | "Here's what I built. Want to tweak anything before sharing?" | "Your tool has been successfully generated! Please review the output." |
| Error state | Honest, helpful | "Hmm, I need a bit more detail. Can you describe what the child should see on screen?" | "Error: Invalid input. Please provide a more specific prompt." |
| Empty state | Encouraging, suggestive | "No tools yet. Describe what your child needs, or browse templates to get started." | "Your workspace is empty. Create your first project to get started." |
| Success/milestone | Warm, genuine | "Done. Your tool is ready to share." | "Congratulations! You've successfully created your first therapy tool! 🎉" |
| Marketing copy | Direct, emotionally resonant | "Your child's therapy tools, built by you." | "AI-powered therapy tool generation platform for enhanced developmental outcomes." |
| Loading/waiting | Reassuring | "Building your tool..." | "Please wait while we process your request." |
| Share prompt | Simple, action-oriented | "Share this tool with one tap." | "Copy the shareable link to distribute this resource to authorized users." |

### Messaging Framework

**Tagline:** "Your child's tools. Built by you."

**Homepage headline:** "Build therapy tools for your child — just describe what you need."

**Value propositions:**
1. **Instantly personalized.** Describe what your child is working on. Get a tool built for their specific goals, their favorite things, their developmental level. Not generic — yours.
2. **No tech skills needed.** If you can describe what you need in a sentence, you can build it. The AI handles everything else. No coding, no configuring, no downloading.
3. **Share with one tap.** Every tool you create works on any phone or tablet. Share it with your therapist, your partner, or another parent — just send the link.

**Objection handlers:**
- "Is this clinically appropriate?" → "Bridges creates practice tools and visual supports, not clinical interventions. Everything it builds is the kind of material therapists already create by hand — visual schedules, communication boards, token boards. We recommend using Bridges alongside your therapy team's guidance."
- "Why not just use an existing app?" → "Existing apps treat every child the same. Your child isn't every child. Bridges creates tools built around their specific goals, interests, and abilities."
- "Is my child's data safe?" → "Bridges doesn't store clinical data or personally identifiable health information. Tools are saved as configurations — images, labels, and layout settings. No therapy notes, no diagnoses, no medical records."

### Elevator Pitches

**5-second:** "Bridges lets parents and therapists build custom autism therapy tools using AI — no coding needed."

**30-second:** "Every kid on the autism spectrum is different, but every therapy app treats them the same. Bridges fixes that. A parent describes what their child is working on — requesting snacks, following a morning routine, practicing new words — and AI builds a personalized, interactive tool in 30 seconds. No coding, no configuring. Just describe what you need and share it with one tap."

**2-minute:** "Here's the problem: ABA therapy and speech therapy work — but they only happen a few hours a week. The other 23 hours a day, parents are on their own. They get sent home with photocopied worksheets and verbal instructions. They download generic apps that don't fit their child's specific goals. Most end up just winging it.

Bridges changes that. It's an AI-powered platform where parents and therapists describe the therapy tools they need in plain language — and get a working, interactive tool in 30 seconds. A parent says 'I need something to help my son practice requesting snacks,' and Bridges builds a communication board with picture cards of his actual favorite foods, a sentence strip, and text-to-speech that says the request out loud. The AI already understands therapy language — you don't have to explain what a token board is or how a visual schedule works.

I'm building this because I live it. My son Ace has Level 1 ASD, and I've seen firsthand how broken the tool landscape is. I'm a full-stack AI developer, so I can build the solution — and the timing is right because AI is finally good enough to understand what a parent means when they say 'morning routine.' We're launching at the Springfield Vibeathon this week and targeting 50 active families in the first 90 days."

### Competitive Differentiation Narrative

The therapy tool landscape has a gap that no existing product fills. On one side: enterprise platforms like CentralReach that serve clinic operations but are impenetrable for parents. On the other: consumer apps like Speech Blubs that are accessible but generic — the same content for every child, disconnected from any real therapy plan.

Bridges sits in the space between. It has the clinical awareness to generate tools that a BCBA would endorse — because it knows what discrete trial training looks like, what a token economy requires, how to structure a visual schedule by complexity level. But it delivers that through an interface a parent can use at 11pm after the kids are in bed — just type what you need in plain language.

The real competitive advantage is domain intelligence. A parent using Replit or Lovable would need to explain what a communication board is, what picture exchange is, how ABA manding works, what reinforcement schedules look like. With Bridges, they just say "help my son request snacks" and the AI fills in the clinical structure. That therapy knowledge base — embedded in the system prompts and RAG pipeline — is what makes the experience feel like magic rather than software.

### Brand Anti-Patterns

**Never feel like hospital software.** No sterile white interfaces with tiny gray text. No form fields labeled "Patient ID" or "Assessment Date." No insurance dropdowns. The moment Bridges feels like an electronic health record, it has failed. Specifically: never use a data-table-heavy UI as the primary view, never show a dashboard with more than 5 elements on first load, never require more than 2 clicks to get to tool creation.

**Never talk down to parents.** Parents of special needs kids are patronized constantly — by schools, by insurance companies, by well-meaning strangers. Bridges does not add to this. No cartoon mascots explaining what a button does. No "Great job!" messages for adult interactions. No simplified language that implies the user can't handle real information. Simplify the interface, not the intelligence of the content.

**Never expose the developer's world.** If a user sees any of these words in the interface, it's a bug: component, API, database, deploy, config, JSON, render, frontend, backend, server, endpoint, token (in the auth sense — "token board" is fine). The technical layer must be completely invisible. Error messages never reference HTTP status codes, database errors, or API failures.

**Never use fake enthusiasm.** No confetti. No 🎉 emoji in success messages. No "You're amazing!" No "Congratulations on creating your first tool!!!" The tone is warm and genuine, not performative. "Done. Your tool is ready to share." is better than "Yay! Your awesome tool is ready! 🎊"

**Never gatekeep the first experience.** No sign-up required before creating a tool. No onboarding quiz. No "Tell us about yourself" before showing value. No pricing page between the user and their first generated tool. The paywall comes after the user has experienced the magic moment, not before.

## 5. Design Direction

### Design Philosophy

**Calm creates confidence.** Every screen should make the user feel like they're in a well-organized, quiet space where things just work. Stress and uncertainty are the default emotional state of our users — a parent worried about their child, a therapist juggling a full caseload. Bridges should be the calmest tool they use all day.

**Big, tappable, forgiving.** Generated therapy tools will be used by adults with one hand on a phone while managing a child with the other hand. And sometimes by children themselves. Every interactive element must be large enough to tap without precision, spaced far enough apart to avoid mis-taps, and forgiving of accidental touches.

**Professional without being cold.** Therapists need to take Bridges seriously as a professional tool. Parents need to feel welcomed, not intimidated. The design walks this line by using clean structure and professional typography while incorporating warm colors and generous breathing room.

**Content over chrome.** The generated therapy tools are the star — not the Bridges UI around them. The builder interface should be minimal and recede, putting maximum visual emphasis on the tool being created and previewed.

### Visual Mood

Clean and trustworthy with subtle warmth. Think of Linear's precision and spatial clarity combined with Calm's soft color palette and breathing room. A white and light gray foundation with a single warm accent color — deep teal — that signals trust without feeling corporate. Minimal borders, subtle shadows, generous whitespace. Typography that's friendly and readable at every size, never clinical or technical.

The energy is composed, not exciting. This isn't a startup trying to generate hype — it's a tool trying to earn trust from people who have been disappointed by technology before. Every visual choice should say: "This was designed thoughtfully. You can rely on it."

### Component Philosophy

**Border radius:** Consistent `--radius: 8px` (Tailwind: `rounded-lg`). Cards, buttons, inputs, modals all use the same radius. Therapy tool components (the generated tools) use `--radius-xl: 16px` for a softer, more child-friendly feel.

**Shadows:** Minimal. Use sparingly for elevation hierarchy only.
- `--shadow-sm: 0 1px 2px rgba(0,0,0,0.05)` — Subtle cards
- `--shadow-md: 0 4px 6px rgba(0,0,0,0.07)` — Elevated panels, popovers
- `--shadow-lg: 0 10px 15px rgba(0,0,0,0.1)` — Modals, floating elements

**Borders:** 1px solid `var(--color-border)`. Used to separate regions, not to decorate. Never use borders on every card — use them to create visual grouping.

**Buttons:**
- Primary: `bg-primary text-white hover:bg-primary-hover` — rounded-lg, px-4 py-2.5, font-medium
- Secondary: `bg-surface border border-border text-foreground hover:bg-surface-raised` — same sizing
- Ghost: `text-primary hover:bg-primary-light` — no border, no background
- Minimum width: 120px for text buttons, 44px for icon buttons

**Inputs:**
- Height: 44px minimum (tap target compliance)
- Border: 1px solid `var(--color-border)`, focus: 2px solid `var(--color-primary)`
- Placeholder text: `var(--color-text-muted)`
- Padding: 12px horizontal

### Iconography & Imagery

**Icon library:** Lucide React (already a shadcn/ui dependency). Outline style, 24px default size, 1.5px stroke width. Use icons to support text, not replace it — every icon in navigation or actions should have an accompanying label.

**Illustration style:** None for MVP. Clean UI without illustrations. If illustrations are added later, they should be simple, warm line drawings — not cartoon characters, not 3D renders, not stock photography.

**Photography:** No stock photography. Ever. Therapy tool picture cards should use clear, simple images — either icons from a curated library or user-uploaded photos. The aesthetic for picture cards in generated tools: flat, clean, high-contrast images on white backgrounds, similar to PECS-style cards.

**What to avoid:** Cartoon mascots, 3D illustrations, gradient-heavy graphics, stock photos of children or families, any imagery that feels clinical or medical.

### Accessibility Commitments

**Target: WCAG 2.1 AA compliance.**

- **Color contrast:** 4.5:1 minimum for normal text, 3:1 for large text. All color combinations verified.
- **Focus indicators:** 2px solid `var(--color-primary)` outline with 2px offset on all interactive elements. Never remove focus outlines.
- **Keyboard navigation:** All interactive elements reachable and operable via keyboard. Logical tab order. Escape closes modals and popovers.
- **Screen readers:** All images have alt text. Form inputs have associated labels. Dynamic content changes announced via aria-live regions. Generated therapy tools include ARIA landmarks.
- **Touch targets:** Minimum 44px x 44px for all tappable elements. Minimum 8px gap between adjacent targets.
- **Reduced motion:** Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. Disable all non-essential animations when this preference is set.
- **Text scaling:** UI remains functional at 200% zoom. No horizontal scrolling at 150% zoom.

### Motion & Interaction

**Philosophy:** Motion should be purposeful and fast. It communicates state changes, guides attention, and provides feedback — it never decorates.

**Default transition:** `150ms ease-out` for hover/focus state changes. CSS: `--transition-fast: 150ms ease-out`.
**Content transitions:** `250ms ease-out` for content appearing/disappearing. CSS: `--transition-normal: 250ms ease-out`.
**Page transitions:** `300ms ease-in-out` for route changes or major view shifts. CSS: `--transition-slow: 300ms ease-in-out`.

**What animates:**
- Hover and focus state changes on buttons and links
- Tooltips and popovers appearing/disappearing
- Chat messages appearing in the conversation (subtle slide-up + fade-in)
- Tool preview rendering (fade-in from 0 to 1 opacity)
- Loading skeletons (shimmer animation)

**What doesn't animate:**
- Page layout shifts
- Data updates in tables or lists
- Color changes on state updates
- Anything that fires more than once per second

**Loading approach:**
- Skeleton screens for initial page loads (match the shape of the expected content)
- Inline spinners for button actions (replace button text with spinner, maintain button width)
- Chat typing indicator for AI responses (three dots pulsing)
- Tool generation: show a shimmer placeholder in the preview area while the AI generates
